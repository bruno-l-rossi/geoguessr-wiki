### Step 1 - Identifying Singapore

![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/0_Infographic__281_29.png)

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/landscape.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/tXHdRe2JM6XexBhd6)

Singapore is a highly developed city-state, characterised by an overall **cleanliness and orderliness**.

Main roads are often divided, and commonly feature black-and-white curbs and double yellow outside lines. You will often see tall apartment blocks nearby.

NOTE: In many cases, this “general look” is all you need to easily identify Singapore. Nevertheless, we have listed some more specific tips below.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/streetsign.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/3gfo9dVeBnTvNSf4A)

All street name signs in Singapore follow this **unique format**. They are green, with rounded edges and a distinct white font.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_License_Plate.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/singapore/Singapore_License_Plate.png)

Most Singaporean licence plates are **black, with white text**. Occasionally, you will be able to see the British style **white-and-yellow** plates and very rarely red plates.

NOTE: Neighbouring Malaysia and Indonesia also use black plates with white text. However, the letters will usually be divided into two or three sections - this is not the case for Singaporean plates.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/directionalsign.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/DwfAaSALd9q2ToKAA)

Directional signs are also **green**, with white letters and English writing.

NOTE: Expressways each have a **three-letter code** (e.g. TPE), making them easy to spot on signs.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Untitled_design__2845_29.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/i6H4a4yEEVqaccW8A)

Road signs in Singapore are unique in that they are always set on a **square white background**.

NOTE: Road signs in almost all other countries will not have this square background.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/raintrees.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/6kBXjMZL9j9apCwn7)

**Rain trees** are seen all around Singapore, and are often planted along main roads.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/roadwarnings.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/CJqw8NSgf2Sttiuy9)

Warnings are often written **on the road** in large letters, such as the famous “HUMP AHEAD”.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Copy_of_Untitled__2822_29.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/TCozcHMoXvMiWzUNA)

Singaporean buses are mostly **lime-green**, making them easily recognisable. Other designs can also be found, for example the [white-and-purple SBS Transit variants](https://goo.gl/maps/SXeRNoTSsTqTmy5BA).

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/busstopbollard.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ZJPL1kB5qake5EF59)

Moreover, all bus stops are protected by **concrete bollards** with unique black-and-yellow **warning bands**.

NOTE: Similar, but not quite identical designs are found in some Malaysian cities.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/busleavingbox.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ghZxZiAc6UtZWKMe6)

In addition to the concrete bollards on bus stops, Singapore sometimes has a unique and yellow **bus-leaving box** with an **arrow** on the street.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/trafficlight.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/7Km9vca4Vp9bjmQb7)

Almost all Singaporean traffic lights follow this design and are supported by a curved **sheet of metal** containing several **holes**.

### Step 2 - Regional clues

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/2022-12-21__283_29.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/amC7LH2dMUiJjRQ4A)

Many streets are named after the neighbourhood they are in, especially in the suburbs.

NOTE: These neighbourhood names appear very quickly on the map, so you can save yourself a lot of time scanning for 5k’s that way. For example, the sign in the image refers to Street 31 in the neighbourhood of Ang Mo Kio.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_Tuas.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/5j6PRBEvEwMorGEj7)

The Tuas planning area is an **industrial area** that is characterised by straight roads, industrial complexes, cranes and shipping containers, making it recognisable.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_Sentosa.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/UE2VjN9xy4LfUbwA8)

You can easily recognise the **island of Sentosa** thanks to the green signposts, winding roads and general resort feel. Occasionally you might see a green lane like [this](https://goo.gl/maps/hrc9MLAc4F6GQihy5) as well.

### Step 3 - Spotlight

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_Pulau-Ubin.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/BXgGkC9vDStCp4jp6)

The Generation 3 **trekker on Pulau Ubin** can be identified by the unusually rural forest coverage and the camouflage-type hat sometimes visible when looking down.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_St_John_and_Lazarus.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/yP92EMgBneCzLgLF9)

You can recognise **St John and Lazarus Islands** thanks to the Generation 3 trekker, the smooth asphalt roads and the sea almost always nearby.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/singapore/Singapore_Kusu_Island.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/RuaECmjmZbgJscC87)

The miniscule Kusu Island is easily identifiable because of the **red paving stones** used for the entire pathway.
