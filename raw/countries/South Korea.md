### Step 1 - Identifying South Korea

![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/0_skr.png)

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/licen_plae.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/south-korea/licen_plae.png)

South Korea uses a variety of different licence plates.

- Pre-2006 plates are short and **green**. These will still show up in the street view coverage.
- Post-2006 plates are **white** for private vehicles, and **yellow** for commercial vehicles. These can be long and short.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_cars.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/tbTZukSFkqn1N1MZ8)

Almost all road coverage in South Korea is made with a **Generation 3** camera.

In most, but not all, of the coverage, the back of the Google car is visible. You can see three colours: **black, white** and **silver**. The car never has an antenna.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_autumn_winter.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/osSQAwfsgfckrB1XA)

A considerable amount of the coverage was made during **autumn and winter**.

Note that more pleasant-looking spring and summer coverage is also not uncommon.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_script.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/south-korea/kr_script.png)

The Korean writing system ([Hangul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangul)) is very unique and recognizable. One of its standout features is the presence of **circles** in many of its letters. This sets it apart from most other East Asian writing systems.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_pole_marking.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/TkNb7qgTUZSZgnUc9)

Utility poles almost always have these pole markings near the bottom, consisting of **black and yellow diagonal stripes**.

These markings look similar to the ones found in Taiwan. There are however two main differences:

- In South Korea, the stripes do not reach all the way to the bottom, whereas in Taiwan they do.
- In South Korea, the stripes are more yellow. In Taiwan, they have more of an orange tint.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_spikey_pole_top.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/prrmMmj6T2xSXCbo7)

Most poles have a tapered “ **spike** ” on top.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_driving_side.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/v4Jk9hdDcM8Wr9tc6)

South Korea drives on the **right**.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_yellow_middle_line.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ZpxGHeTxVh1PJPkz9)

Roads generally have **yellow middle lines** and white outer lines.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_red_block_markings.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/9vPs9pKCLm6Uamza7)

You can sometimes see **red block markings** on the road.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_yellow_road_signs.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/5jqQBFFuqsHZKpFv6)

Triangular warning signs have a **yellow** **background**.

Note that circular prohibition signs, such as speed signs, have a white background. Yield signs also have a white background.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_bluestreetsigns.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/vY72thgUtoGCszoP9)

Street signs will be blue, **arrow-shaped** and commonly **hanging** from a metal bar mounted to utility poles.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_directionsigns.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/hNg8VMHZfqwncfy36)

Direction signs in South Korea have white text on a green background.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_pedestrian_crossing.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/NSKv8YjTzn1ZbJQ66)

Speed bumps are often painted **yellow and white**.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_pedestriansigns.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/k47ebKQ4Gw79EuA79)

South Korean pedestrian crossing signs can be easily recognised from the distinct **pentagonal shape** combined with the blue background.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_house_number.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/2GHGKRZix5fWNu586)

Korean buildings have very distinctive **house numbers**. They are blue and usually are shaped quite literally like a house. The full address of the building is written on them, in both Hangul and Latin script.

Some places have unique house numbers, which you can use to region-guess. More information about this can be found in the spotlight section.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_regular_bollards.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/eQxAo1Wsp27Uijsd6)

You will sometimes see these black and white wedge-shaped bollards. They have a **round yellow reflector** on the front, and a grey one on the back.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_mountains.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Gj8yUq1ZqxRonkit8)

The South Korean landscape is predominantly **mountainous**. It is very rare to not see at least some hills or mountains in the distance. Even in the flat areas, you can almost always see elevation on the horizon.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_brick.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/UjfepaaR8g7rJWdm9)

**Red brick** is a very common building material in South Korea.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_roofs.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/7oS2zUZX1Pzd2djo8)

Many buildings have **traditional tiled roofs** like these. These often have decorative elements on the corners, and tend to have sides that curve slightly upwards. Blue roofs are quite common.

### Step 2 - Region-specific clues

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_coveragemap.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/south-korea/kr_coveragemap.png)

South Korea has fairly limited and **very spotty coverage**. Other than large highways, most of the coverage is concentrated in and around major cities.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_phonecodes%20v4.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/south-korea/kr_phonecodes.png)

**Area codes** in South Korea increase to the south, from 02 in the north, to 064 on Jeju. It is also notable that Seoul has the only two-digit area code in the country.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_gen2.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@35.2380562,129.0137196,3a,90y,173.36h,88.35t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s6ChCjLvrWfAixJSzJTDwsg!2e0!7i13312!8i6656?entry=ttu)

**Generation 2** coverage is almost exclusively found in Seoul and Busan. It can also be found on a couple of highways outside the cities, however almost all of that coverage has been overwritten.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_carmeta.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@36.0162173,129.5038044,3a,27.9y,346.77h,57.84t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sovkxxZFe9VslU8BLU4CyDQ!2e0!7i13312!8i6656?entry=ttu)

A **small camera** can be seen mounted above the brake light on the Street View car on coverage northeast of Seoul and around Pohang.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_jejudirt.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/NmJ6Uhd37RMPhN1NA)

Jeju is a **volcanic island** with a lot of dark volcanic dirt, where you can also often see volcanoes in the distance.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_jejurockwall.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/U1rumCx5sPaMssQbA)

Jeju also has a lot of **rock walls**, made of **dark** volcanic rocks.

### Step 3 - Spotlight

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_gimjeplains.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/1tqULULv3YHhS8SP7)

The area around Gimje can be recognised by being one of the only **completely flat** areas of South Korea, combined with the open, **yellow-green fields** along almost every road in the area.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/south-korea/kr_hampyeonghousenumber.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Fzkc7Sf4kW3UkNqo7)

House numbers in the Hampyeong region can be recognised from the **large white section** at the top of the sign.
