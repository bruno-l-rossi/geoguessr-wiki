### Step 1 - Identifying Mexico

![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/transparent-mx_summary_2026.png)

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_octopole.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/kpKmy54dU5U8GHT68)

Most utility poles in Mexico are made of concrete and have an **octagonal shape**. There will often be engravings on the side.

NOTE: Octagonal poles are also common in Colombia.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_licenceplate.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/P3se454FAsG1wL627)

Mexico uses short licence plates with a large variety of designs. Front plates are required.

NOTE: For an overview of the most common regional designs, see step 2.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_roadlines.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/pdBPetpke4XTrHZd9)

The most typical Mexican road lines are white solid outer lines with a **single unbroken yellow middle line**.

NOTE: [Single](https://goo.gl/maps/Dbjb5sJ1xcrQBTof7) broken yellow middle lines and [double](https://goo.gl/maps/L9YSqFc6h79idwkm8) yellow middle lines are also common.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_bollard.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/KhHGYom36sLoUwKv5)

These small, **cylindrical** white bollards with a **black base** are unique to Mexico. A yellow reflector can sometimes be seen, as well as a flat version.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_roadshields.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/pNcF4kHAFfqPzvZZ9)

Mexico has two general road shields; one for national highways with “MEXICO” written at the top and a slightly curvy design, and one for state roads which have straight borders, and typically has the state initials written at the top.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_directionalsign.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/RQu2i5MEW6oDiyTPA)

Mexican directional signs have a distinctly large, **all-caps** white font.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_kmmarker.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/kdz5s51wJask1Vi58)

Mexican **kilometre markers** are white, with black font and outline.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cacti.jpg)](https://goo.gl/maps/EWB6iUQ7GYnDzLa57)

**Cacti**, of many different species, are extremely common in Mexico.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_alto.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/rSjYzhXbWgrhjA2dA)

Mexico uses the word “ALTO” on stop signs.

NOTE: All of South America uses the word “PARE”.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_arrow.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/JeXWbEdyJst8diRh6)

Arrows on signs are noticeably **very thick**.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_yellowkerbs.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/sCAekuAL7j5niBM8A)

**Yellow** accents on kerbs and bridge barriers are quite common.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_watertanks.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/7LjRWpcvW4jHBkmB9)

You will commonly see wide cylindrical, **black or white water tanks** on rooftops.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_brands.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/zRUiMrqgaYkDDNuFA)

**OXXO** and **Pemex** are two of the most popular gas station brands in Mexico.

NOTE: While less common, OXXO can still be found in [other countries](https://www.google.com/maps/@-33.4481518,-70.5764202,3a,15y,199.57h,89.44t,1.18r/data=!3m7!1e1!3m5!1sJaAVdAE4oby9-yp1rsx3PQ!2e0!5s20230501T000000!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu).

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_placenames.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/d5ha7Vsp3zsYPDVz7)

Many place names in Mexico are of [Nahuatl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahuatl) origin, featuring a lot of ‘x’ and ‘tl’ as well as ‘-ec’ and ‘-tlan’ suffixes.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_electricitycounter.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/JNiHkfHBfje1yGRe8)

Mexico uses **circular** electricity counters.

NOTE: Circular electricity counters are not unique to Mexico and can also be found in the US, Guatemala, Dominican Republic and the Philippines.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_americancars.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/RpDyz3Ya1RRmMAk56)

United States car brands are popular in Mexico. In particular, you will see a lot of **pickup trucks**.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_gen2.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/P5zUcpVycv8rnRnj8)

Mexico is one of very few Latin American countries that has **Generation 2 coverage**.

NOTE: Brazil has some Generation 2, but it is considerably more rare, as most of it is overwritten.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_gen3car.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/CTGk6hsrdmPc8Mrg6)

The Generation 3 coverage in Mexico mainly feature two cars:

- A white car with a **stubby antenna**. There is a diagonal coil wrapped around the antenna.
- A white car with a more **visible** back and a long red **brake light** instead of an antenna.

It is possible to find a white car without a stubby antenna, but with a tiny [sliver](https://goo.gl/maps/qxynyEHgmp85RBmu5) visible where the antenna should be.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_gen4cars2.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/mexico/mx_gen4cars2.png)

The Generation 4 cars in Mexico commonly have a visible front and can feature various different colour patterns. The three most common ones are:

- Blue
- White with a blue stripe
- Black

### Step 2 - Region-specific clues

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_areacodes_nobg.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/mexico/mx_areacodes_nobg.png)

The first digit of Mexican **area codes** are regional and can be seen on this map.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_postcodes.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/mexico/mx_postcodes.png)

Similarly, Mexican **postal codes** can be seen on this map. They will commonly appear at the [bottom of street signs](https://goo.gl/maps/8bgZx7q8pjzzFSYi6), and are ordered alphabetically based on state names.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_licenceplates.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/mexico/mx_licenceplates.png)

Every state in Mexico has one or several unique licence plate designs. In this infographic you can find the most common and helpful ones.

#### Vegetation

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_pricklypear.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@20.0493174,-99.0107792,3a,56.8y,272.7h,93.27t,0.35r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s_Fs2LO1LEepCqlWqsHaO2Q!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Prickly pear cacti** are distinct with their **flat oval-shaped pads**. They are noticeably more common centrally and are rarely seen in southern and coastal states.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_pulqueagave.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/eESXKSV8dxt54UNL8)

Agave salmiana, also known as **pulque agaves**, have wide, smooth, curling leaves, and a **crown-like** appearance. They are common in the central Mexican valley and further north around the city of San Luis Potosi.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_tequilafield.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@20.8753454,-103.7311886,3a,90y,101.01h,87.56t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s4xdYQ4A6doHYwdLgqVL3Qg!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

Vast fields of **blue agave** (Agave tequilana), used to produce tequila, are mostly found in Jalisco and Nayarit.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_treeyucca.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/j9E1p4WQtykwYAeT6)

Yucca filifera, also known as **tree yucca**, as well as yucca decipiens are most commonly seen in San Luis Potosi and Zacatecas as well as some of the bordering northern states. They can be recognised by their oblong rosettes of sharp green leaves branching from a single trunk, similar to a tree.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_bajacacti.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/U2pgMhGoUJopPGgs6)

The **Mexican Giant Cardon** is an extremely common cacti in the Baja California peninsula. It can be identified through its large sprawling arms stemming from a singular trunk and ribbed exterior.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_saguarocacti.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@?api=1&map_action=pano&pano=F9TxZXH5v35cO354YEawHw&viewpoint=31.757994,-112.870238&heading=263.5458556766323&pitch=2.5053923868921117&fov=17.839695441002988&shorturl=1#extra%5BloadMode%5D=latLng)

The **Saguaro** **Cacti** can be spotted in the western half of Sonora, featuring short curving arms that stem from a main trunk.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_organpipecacti.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/zBZ7EhGGYqZcdHYu9)

**Organ Pipe Cacti** have long thin stems that often come out from a stem that is around ground level. They are most commonly found in Sonora and occasionally in Baja California Sur.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_bluemyrtlecacti.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@20.7071168,-100.7817116,3a,43.8y,348.83h,94.28t,1.4r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s0EhedSJ_t2vTNRVEUTFw_w!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

The **Blue Myrtle Cacti** have short arms that uniformly curve and point upwards. It is abundantly found in San Luis Potosi, but also common in Queretaro and Hidalgo.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_sugarcanecacti.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@22.1823547,-99.2254701,3a,75.6y,99.52h,87.1t,2.59r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s589n3ZLFIq6j9yZ64_Pecg!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Sugar cane** can be found all over the country, but by far most common in Veracruz.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_coconutpalm.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@?api=1&map_action=pano&pano=hXqBjOCPbTjY6oYGJwHwzg&viewpoint=18.949149,-104.123361&heading=332.03729498683276&pitch=7.964087451297019&fov=25.973071648944725&shorturl=1)

**Coconut palms** can be found all across the coast, but are often found in large groups in Guerrero and Colima.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_sabalpalm.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@22.2483121,-105.2673387,3a,24.7y,231.1h,95.1t,0.55r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sfFlVUMFSHorwh8PjKcf52Q!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Sabal palms** are most commonly observed in the east coast of Mexico, the Yucatan peninsula, as well as the state of Nayarit. They feature a spiky, sun-shaped crown, giving the tree a **lollipop-like** appearance.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_veracruzpalm.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/86E641kokWtYN5NX6)

The **Attalea rostrata** is a huge palm recognizable from its upright leaves, almost resembling an umbrella. It is mostly exclusive to Veracruz and Tabasco, and can be rarely found in Chiapas.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_oilpalm.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@17.6968982,-92.2806729,3a,75y,352.6h,84.86t/data=!3m7!1e1!3m5!1s11lQZwsDCvfykm1S4tql2Q!2e0!6shttps:%2F%2Fstreetviewpixels-pa.googleapis.com%2Fv1%2Fthumbnail%3Fpanoid%3D11lQZwsDCvfykm1S4tql2Q%26cb_client%3Dmaps_sv.tactile.gps%26w%3D203%26h%3D100%26yaw%3D77.96164%26pitch%3D0%26thumbfov%3D100!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Oil palm plantations** are most common in Chiapas, and occasionally on the outskirts of the state in Veracruz, Tabasco, and Campeche.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_boojumtree.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@30.0582843,-115.3339231,3a,21.9y,138.83h,103.69t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sjQxWhh9epjKO7Ze2KpTplw!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Boojum trees** can be recognized by their alien, **tentacle** -like appearance. They are almost exclusively found in the southern half of Baja California.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_brittlebush.png)](https://maps.app.goo.gl/upX4KjV2JhraLmsw6)

**Brittlebush** are small shrubs with bright **silver-grey** leaves that are most commonly found in Sonora, especially around Hermosillo.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_quintanarootrees.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@19.6446686,-88.5994485,3a,45.1y,194.5h,97.43t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sB_stle2_Fal_jhAMboX2nw!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

These **thin, white-barked** trees can be found in the Yucatan peninsula, but are most prevalent in Quintana Roo.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_coahuilagrass.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/kMghJ7zmTaMFM85t9)

These **gold tufts** of grass in a line right next to the road are most commonly found in Coahuila.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_tlaxcalagrass.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/HqCNnDAhY6UJzUSr8)

These **clumps** of grass in almost a teardrop shape are common in the region around Tlaxcala.

#### Landscapes

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_zacatecasredfield.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/rQYm3miw7JatszdW6)

**Red agricultural fields** are commonly seen in Zacatecas and occasionally southern Durango.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_monterreymountains.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/7Bh1qNJ77XSgQcju7)

The Monterrey metropolitan area features tall stunning **mountain ranges** that surround the city.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_northernpineforest.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/hmtV3uhgYJ3TKxb27)

The western parts of Chihuahua and Durango can feature a rocky mountainous landscape covered with **pine forests**. In general, these pines are shorter and more **stunted** in appearance than pine forests of the central or south.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_guanajuatodarkfields.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@20.6407472,-101.4633552,3a,68.3y,9.86h,83.47t,1.68r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1s39n40IFSrD5dgb4EFxTJBw!2e0!7i13312!8i6656?entry=ttu)

**Extremely dark fields** are common in Guanajuato.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_bajahills.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/38GATidM5TLUZAqMA)

These **reddish brown-tinged** hills, often accompanied by **large boulders**, are a common sight for the northern half of Baja California.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_whitesoilqroo.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@19.362134,-88.9422351,3a,69.8y,257.2h,70.1t,1.27r/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1srCTt4rkxctIIZxa31rDm9A!2e0!7i13312!8i6656?entry=ttu)

Almost **blindingly white** soil in the Yucatan peninsula is most common in Quintana Roo.

NOTE: This is only consistent in Generation 3.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_campecheagriculture.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Q2WkYKstD146TXjy9)

These **red agricultural fields** surrounded by dense forests of tropical trees are most common in northern Campeche.

#### Architecture

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_redbrick.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/GZJE1MZpqxTRarqQ8)

**Red brick** buildings are mostly concentrated in the states west of Mexico City, specifically Jalisco, Michoacan, Guanajuato, and Aguascalientes.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_whitebrick.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/PdoQRtuWFJr6DV4i7)

**White concrete block** buildings are highly concentrated in the states surrounding Mexico city, and are generally more common to the east. They can however still be found all over the country.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_mudbrick.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ZvQrEzzGVoLuV9xM8)

**Mud brick** buildings are most abundant in Durango, especially full houses constructed with mud brick. However, they can essentially be found in almost all of the dry desert states.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_northernarchitecture.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/HmbhoDg17PFT3XUA7)

Architecture in northern Mexico often features design elements from American style bungalows. Many houses are coloured with **light** or **pastel** colours, **gated** and commonly have front yards. **Orange tiles** inspired by Spanish colonial architecture are also typical.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_thatchedhuts.png)](https://maps.app.goo.gl/x1bgEPqzNYHHyVZPA)

**Thatched roofs** are most common in the Yucatan peninsula and eastern San Luis Potosi. They can also be found elsewhere, mainly in the southeast.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_yucatanrockwalls.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/KchYSZZ2LaetmrHs9)

These walls made of **light coloured rock** are found in the Yucatan peninsula, but most common in Yucatan state.

#### Infrastructure

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_chihuahuacross.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Xzc72AMpvXw7C5zAA)

A thick **cross** can be seen on the back of signs in Chihuahua.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_nayaritbollard.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/YLJa6XxeD4afNhmb6)

Bollards with a **red band** near the top are unique to Nayarit, apart from [one exception](https://www.google.com/maps/@16.416407,-94.109672,3a,15y,95.28h,64.73t,0.03r/data=!3m7!1e1!3m5!1s_lRtg1MKWPSNX_dJch3kig!2e0!5s20220501T000000!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu) being Federal Highway 190 on the Oaxaca-Chiapas border.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_jaliscobollard.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/f3SygmWwo5HUfFkN8)

Jalisco bollards will sometimes have a **crown** and the text ‘ **Jalisco** ’ written on it. These are usually barely legible unless very close, however no other Mexican bollard will have writing on it.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_coastalinsulators.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/mexico/mx_coastalinsulators.png)

**White insulators** that are shaped like acorns can be found along the coastal states of Mexico, but are by far most common in Michoacan, Colima, and Veracruz.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_asymmetricpoles.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/1UcPNupGtVZJb7VG9)

Poles with an **asymmetric crossbar** and **double-stacked** insulators are exclusive to Mexico City, State of Mexico, Hidalgo, and Morelos.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_sinaloapole.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/bAnLnCP38Ht29cVV7)

**Wooden** poles with a **lowered crossbar** are mostly found in Sinaloa and Sonora.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_yucatanhangingpoles.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/HgVE2AHqrxEZNYkXA)

These concrete poles with insulators **suspended below** the crossbars are unique to the Yucatan peninsula.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_bajahangingpole.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/XGkAQS8FKC3GoDQU7)

The **wooden** variant of the hanging pole is found on the Baja California peninsula.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_durangopole.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Qjo483zUY68QJ6UBA)

Poles with a lowered crossbar and a **single support beam** that extend from one end of the crossbar to the pole itself can be found in Durango.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_meshpole.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@21.8385263,-100.9159054,3a,50.1y,82.64h,101.74t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sfgrFLxJCgUrvB28Z3w1SQw!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

Poles with a **mesh body** are most commonly found in San Luis Potosi, Guanajuato, and Queretaro.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_tabascowhitelines.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/tEL5f7GE4BA2UNp86)

**White** middle roadlines are most commonly found in Tabasco.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_veracruzfence.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/Be6FBU236REnZ3No9)

These fences made out of trees with **red-coloured bark** are commonly found in Veracruz.

NOTE: These tree fences can still be found in other coastal states and are best utilised when combined with other clues.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_blacktarroadlines.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@24.7770873,-103.634008,3a,39.3y,336.07h,71.03t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sGxYsoGoT29w3TZn2boCHVA!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

**Black tar** on both sides of the yellow centre line can be seen in Sinaloa, Durango, and Guerrero.

NOTE: This is most reliable when the tar is not faded.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_northeastpavement.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/EQzJ8Xnwu45oRAnB7)

**Grainy** pavement with a **blue-ish grey tinge** is a common sight for the states of Tamaulipas and Nuevo Leon.

NOTE: In Tamaulipas, larger roads very commonly feature [dashed outer lines](https://goo.gl/maps/J82S2uF1oZ7qGhEG6).

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_queretaropavement.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/vPfkMSqKsZdmnruU8)

This style of **cobblestone pavement**, unorganised with irregular tiles, can be found in most of the central states, but is by far most commonly found in the states of Nayarit, Jalisco, Colima, and Queretaro.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_pueblapavement.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/D86HoNCxFCWcn4Ri7)

This pavement consisting of **hexagonal tiles** is found in Puebla and Tlaxcala.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_oaxacapavement.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/YRCQVsJzgDmTrgrp6)

This exact pavement design with **three lines** of cobblestone patterning is mainly found in Oaxaca.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cdmxtaxi.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/WDN13v76j41pCMsM7)

Taxis in and around Mexico City have a white lower half with a **pink upper half**.

### Step 3 - Spotlight

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_mexicali.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ZgUza1DK5fP8FB828)

The area around **Mexicali** features flat farmland with a ridge to the west. [Washingtonia](https://goo.gl/maps/Z9JhouH2WnYeJW1z8) [palms](https://goo.gl/maps/Z9JhouH2WnYeJW1z8) are very common and these [poles](https://goo.gl/maps/JG3LABTi1T4aSrzc9) made out of brown wood can be found.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_pozaricaorangetrees.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/SauarFbZNgn3gtjt5)

The area around Poza Rica in Veracruz features a lot of **citrus tree plantations**. It is common to see large amounts of these trees grown right on top of the hills.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_ciudadobregoncanals.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/FfZbnMLXubMnGAe26)

The area around Ciudad Obregon is distinct due to the **pinkish-tan soil** and **canals** found around the city’s agricultural areas.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_twinvolcanoe.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@19.0752039,-98.7925,3a,49.3y,83.77h,94.56t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sNzXT96SWntP3juhVhmTIYw!2e0!7i13312!8i6656?entry=ttu)

The **twin volcanoes** of Iztaccíhuatl and Popocatépetl are found on the border between the State of Mexico and Puebla. Keep in mind that they are [usually much less visible](https://goo.gl/maps/3zngjfu2ucdDwvcV6) than depicted in the image, which is just for reference.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cerrodelbernal.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/dyhptXhwihUA4BF78)

**Cerro del Bernal** is a distinct extinct volcano that is found in southern Tamaulipas. It can be found heading directly northwest of Tampico or west of Morón.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_sierradelabratanchipa.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/9URq6JATzzPEEZLo9)

This extremely **long** and **flat ridge** is found in the Sierra del Abra Tanchipa Biosphere Reserve in eastern San Luis Potosi.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_coaridge.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/fAyWYykitQzwJTZ27)

This tall, flat ridge, with very **prominent ridgelines**, belongs to the Maderas del Carmen Biosphere Reserve in Northern Coahuila near the US border.

NOTE: The only road the mountain range can be observed from is the Federal Highway 53 that leads to Boquillas del Carmen.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_ciudadjuarez.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@31.543849,-106.5117097,3a,90y,352.18h,92.14t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sIrrDAY7r24Dnlp19oqKV4A!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

The outskirts of Ciudad Juárez features a lot of dirt roads with a distinct shade of **orange soil** and **low shrubs**.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_oaxacapinkwhitesoil.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@17.6210362,-97.3792943,3a,90y,248.33h,93.09t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sfGHMkVZzVonG-3vejsTGNA!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

The area around San Cristóbal Suchixtlahuaca and south of it in Oaxaca often feature rolling hills with really distinct **orange** and **beige** -tinged soil, passing from one colour to the other with each hill. The beige-tinged soil can be seen more [prominent](https://goo.gl/maps/HzfgXbCXgYegsKodA) in some locations.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_guaymas.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/cEf7qFKQ47p7N8nAA)

The town of Guaymas has a lot of **jagged** cliffs and mountains that make the area very distinct. The soil is also distinctly pinkish and organ-pipe cacti can be seen scattered on the hills and next to the road.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_loscoloradaspinklake.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/g1brv2ksYhUaeryy7)

The town of Los Coloradas is famously known for the **pink lakes** around the area.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_54dividedyuccaroad.png)](https://www.google.com/maps/@23.7297003,-101.8587715,3a,90y,61.88h,92.46t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1sD7yTNt_m3HafD7awIHrkiw!2e0!7i16384!8i8192?entry=ttu)

The 54 is a divided highway that runs through northeastern Zacatecas which features **yucca trees** spammed on both sides of the road. Additionally, the streetview car is often exposed and appears almost [white](https://goo.gl/maps/BfA6FLRVcL371P9z9) due to the sunlight.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_40d.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ne4Ak8ZWUBZokKfC8)

The 40D that crosses the Sinaloa-Durango state line is recognizable by the stunning lush **cliffs** and mountains along the road. The highway commonly passes through tunnels and the road itself features **double yellow** middle roadlines.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_95Ddivider.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/ytU4jhGEMJNAEyJp9)

This road divider with a **yellow** **top** and **bottom** edge is found all along the 95D from Mexico City to Acapulco.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_euruswindfarm.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/n2C8v7axgihTXxEH7)

The Eurus Wind Farm is located in southeast Oaxaca and is the largest wind farm in Mexico. Rows of **wind turbines** can be found along the 190 around Juchitán de Zaragoza.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_aguascalientesyellowlamppost.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/zCHUgsRQHFMSqDCf7)

Most lamp posts and lamp arms in Aguascalientes city are **yellow**.

NOTE: They are **not** exclusive and are only very common.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cuernavacalamp.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/NUMqrHa8EygE2AcJ6)

This lamp with a **black** rectangular head is found in Cuernavaca.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_santiagodequeretarolamp.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/h21ok4dySUXkqWVk8)

This lamp with black front and back **edges** and a white middle section is found in Santiago de Queretaro.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cancunlamp.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/MQLpUCqKccy3HpfJA)

This lamp with **five** circular bulbs that looks like a **dice** is found in Cancun.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_matamoroslamp.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/EFqBhvX12UT4pqRF8)

This lamp with a rectangular head and a **ring-shaped** light bulb is found in Matamoros.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_hermosillolamp.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/q9NXWaDuqsjSGonYA)

This lamp with a **zig-zag** bent pole arm is found in Hermosillo.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cuernavacagreenpolepaint.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/U334CG4baEuLWpeX8)

The bottom half of poles in Cuernavaca are often painted **green**.

NOTE: In Generation 4, the paint usually looks faded, some to the point where it is barely visible.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_chihuahuacitypole.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/69rSVB5bnrT5vrrt8)

Poles in Chihuahua city occasionally have a strip of **white pole paint** with the street name written in black or blue text.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_lamalinchecar.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/XtUGG2rA1LXx7Sz56)

This Street View car with a **yellow hood** can be found by the La Malinche volcano in Tlaxcala.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_cofredeperotecar.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/k1UHPYNV5ed8HQK3A)

This **white truck** with visible rearview mirrors can be seen in the Cofre de Perote national park.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/mexico/mx_lasecacar.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/VZYPyGzKtz3khqAB6)

This **red** Street View car with a black roof can be found in the town of La Seca in Sinaloa.
