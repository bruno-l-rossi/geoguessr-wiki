### Step 1 - Identifying Belarus

![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/0_summary_belarus.png)

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/Coverage_map.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/belarus/Coverage_map.png)

The only coverage in Belarus, apart from spills, is a single **walking trekker** in the historic centre of Minsk.

Instead of learning a lot of country-specific clues, it is more practical to simply memorise what the trekker looks like.

You can recognise trekkers by the fact that the camera feels a bit lower than the usual Google car. In Belarus’ case, the trekker is also mostly on pedestrian-only roads.

*You can find information about the border spill coverage in the [Spillover Guide](https://www.plonkit.net/spillover-countries).*

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/General_look.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/T2dS9DmPMAHvoG7s7)

The Minsk trekker features a **historic part of the city**, with well-maintained classical European architecture. Many buildings have white or pastel coloured walls. Most of the roads you see are pedestrian-only.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/church.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/knpeW7A115CrHQaZ9)

The Holy Spirit Cathedral features prominently in the coverage. Its two towers can often be seen from far away.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/by_pavement.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/GsTiY3PefHnHorth9)

The pavement which the trekker walks on in Minsk is mostly **grey rectangular tiles**. On a small part, there are [pink-ish tiles](https://goo.gl/maps/jBJvXJYu2yqYENLw9) in a specific pattern.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/by_language.png)](https://www.plonkit.net/images/belarus/by_language.png)

Signs will be in Belarusian or Russian. Both languages use the Cyrillic alphabet.

NOTE: The letters ў and і are not found in Russian.

[![](https://www.plonkit.net/images/resize/1920/80/belarus/Street_signs.png)](https://goo.gl/maps/SCKDqSV1qcotwwbFA)

The Minsk centre street signs are **sea-green**, and can be seen almost everywhere.

NOTE: If you are close enough to the sign, you can make out the Cyrillic letters “вул (vul)”, the Belarusian name for “street”.
